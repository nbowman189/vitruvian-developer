Act 1: The Foundation

I was sitting in a soils lab in Iraq, crunching soil composition numbers on Army forms. My job as a Technical Engineer Specialist was precision work‚Äîsurveying, drafting, soils analysis. When the numbers were wrong, people could get hurt.

So naturally, I looked at the manual process we were using and thought: This is a system. And every system has failure points.

The Army had specific forms we'd fill out by hand. Numbers written, math calculated, all points of potential error. I had a background in Excel, so I did what any engineer would do: I built a better system. I recreated the forms digitally, automating the calculations, removing the human error from the equation.

After the military, my path wasn't a straight line to a server room. Instead, I pursued a Bachelor of Arts in History with minors in Classical Civilizations and Religious Studies. Studying history taught me how complex, self-managing systems are organized. The Romans had a sophisticated architecture of governance, but when the system developed cascading failures, the empire fell‚Äînot because of a single event, but because the architecture became unstable at its core.

This wasn't just history. It was a masterclass in scalable systems, external pressures, and the critical importance of a stable core architecture. I was learning the ability to see entire systems, not just immediate problems.

Act 2: The Detour

With a History degree, I took an unexpected path into teaching Exceptional Student Education. There were moments of incredible reward‚Äîseeing a student succeed because someone believed in them.

But I began recognizing something critical: I was solving the wrong problem. I was competent as a teacher, but I was building someone else's success on foundations I hadn't yet laid for myself. The teaching environment had too many uncontrollable variables‚Äîand I needed to return to work where I could design systems instead of manage chaos.

As my focus shifted, another realization emerged: I was engineering success for others, but I had failed to engineer my own health.

My father had a negative health event. When I looked at the trajectory of his life‚Äîhis choices in food, his approach to fitness‚ÄîI saw how poor systems lead to cascading failures. It was a pattern recognition moment. The same principle I saw in Rome, in empires, in database architecture‚Äîit applied to human health too.

I returned to technology. I progressed through three levels‚Äîhelpdesk to Solutions Engineer to Sr. Data Solutions Engineer‚Äîin four years. But this time was different. I now had two parallel projects: my career, and my health.
